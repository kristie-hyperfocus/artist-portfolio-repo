Earth & Material

Clay dug from the hills of Alentejo. Ochres and iron oxides gathered by hand from Portuguese riverbeds. Nothing sourced from a catalogue.

02

Fire & Form

Every piece wood-fired over 24–36 hours. The flame decides the surface. Sol controls the shape; the kiln controls everything else.

03

Mark & Surface

Earth pigment painting begins after firing. Ochre and ash mixed with beeswax and rabbit-skin glue — techniques unchanged for centuries.

The Artist

Sol Maren

Sol Maren grew up between Oaxaca and Lisbon, shaped by the ceramics traditions of both. She trained in Faenza, Italy, then returned to establish a wood-fire studio in the hills above the Tagus estuary.

Her work moves between vessels and earth pigment painting — objects and surfaces that share the same material logic: taken from the ground, transformed by heat, returned to the hand.

What Feeds the Work

Process & Influence

"The materials have been here for ten thousand years. I'm just learning to ask them the right questions."

Sol reads widely in geology, botany, and traditional craft. Her studio walls are covered with soil samples, mineral specimens, and botanical drawings. She keeps a notebook of pigment recipes and records every firing in a handwritten journal that dates back to 2011.

The biggest influence is the land itself — the red soil of the Alentejo, the silver-green of the olive trees, the particular light of the Tagus estuary in winter. She says: "I'm not interpreting nature. I'm using nature to describe itself."
